Phishing Threat Analysis

An analysis of phishing indicators, suspicious websites, attack types, and response actions in a simulated environment.

Overview

In this project, I analyzed phishing threats and identified the warning signs that help users and analysts recognize suspicious emails, fake websites, and social engineering attempts. The goal was to understand how phishing works, how to detect it, and what actions should be taken to reduce risk.

Note: Developed as part of a cybersecurity internship using a simulated environment.

Objective

  • Identify phishing indicators in emails and websites
  • Understand common phishing attack types
  • Improve detection of social engineering threats
  • Practice response steps used in security operations

Methodology

  • Reviewed common phishing techniques
  • Identified red flags in emails and websites
  • Categorized phishing attack types
  • Documented safe response actions
  • Connected phishing detection to SOC responsibilities

Email Red Flags

Sender and Domain Mismatch Spoofed display names, lookalike domains, and unusual sender addresses.
Urgency and Pressure Messages designed to force quick action before the user can verify.
Suspicious Content Requests for passwords, financial details, or unexpected verification.
Links and Attachments Hidden URLs, shortened links, or unexpected files.

Suspicious Website Red Flags

  • Misspelled or lookalike URLs
  • No HTTPS or weak trust indicators
  • Fake login pages
  • Broken formatting or poor design
  • Requests for excessive personal information

Phishing Types Identified

Email Phishing Broad email campaigns targeting many users at once.
Spear Phishing More targeted attacks using personal or organizational details.
Whaling Attacks aimed at executives or leadership.
Smishing / Vishing Phishing through text messages or phone calls.
QR Code Phishing QR codes leading users to malicious websites.
Business Email Compromise Fraudulent requests that imitate trusted business contacts.

Key Findings

  • Phishing often relies on urgency, trust, and confusion rather than technical complexity
  • Email and website indicators can reveal phishing attempts before harm occurs
  • Users are a critical part of security defense
  • Fast reporting reduces the impact of suspicious messages and links

Response Guidance

  1. Do not click links or open attachments
  2. Preserve evidence with screenshots or saved messages
  3. Report the message to the correct contact
  4. If a link was clicked, notify support immediately
  5. If credentials were entered, change passwords right away
  6. Delete the message after documentation and reporting

SOC Analyst Relevance

Phishing is one of the most common issues handled by SOC teams. Analysts must investigate suspicious emails, identify indicators of phishing, escalate confirmed threats, and support containment after user interaction.

  • Suspicious sender and link mismatch → possible phishing alert
  • Credential submission after fake login page → possible account compromise
  • Business email compromise attempt → fraud and escalation risk

What I Learned
