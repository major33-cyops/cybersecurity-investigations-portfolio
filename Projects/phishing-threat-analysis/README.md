# Phishing Threat Analysis

## Overview
In this project, I analyzed phishing threats and identified the warning signs that help users and analysts recognize suspicious emails, fake websites, and social engineering attacks. The goal was to understand how phishing works, how to detect it, and how to respond effectively to reduce risk.

## Key Threat Indicators

### Email Indicators
- Suspicious or spoofed sender addresses  
- Misspelled or lookalike domains (e.g., paypaI.com)  
- Urgent or threatening language  
- Unexpected attachments or links  
- Generic greetings (e.g., “Dear Customer”)  
- Requests for sensitive information  

### Website Indicators
- Fake login pages designed to steal credentials  
- Misspelled or deceptive URLs (e.g., amaz0n-login.com)  
- Missing HTTPS or browser security warnings  
- Poor design, broken links, or low-quality formatting  
- Requests for excessive personal information

## Phishing Attack Types Identified
- Email phishing  
- Spear phishing  
- Whaling  
- Smishing (SMS phishing)  
- Vishing (voice phishing)  
- QR code phishing  
- Business Email Compromise (BEC)  

---

## Key Findings
- Phishing relies heavily on urgency, trust, and human behavior  
- Small inconsistencies in emails and websites often reveal attacks  
- Users play a critical role in preventing security incidents  
- Fast reporting significantly reduces risk and impact  

---

## Example Scenario
A user receives an email stating their bank account is locked and must be verified immediately.

Steps taken:
1. User checks sender email and notices irregularities  
2. Hovers over link and identifies a suspicious URL  
3. Does not click the link  
4. Reports the email as phishing  
5. Deletes the message  

---

## Response Guidance
If a phishing attempt is suspected:
1. Do not click links or open attachments  
2. Preserve evidence (screenshots or saved email)  
3. Report the message to the appropriate contact  
4. If a link was clicked, notify support immediately  
5. If credentials were entered, reset passwords  
6. Remove the message after reporting  

---

## SOC Analyst Relevance
Phishing is one of the most common security threats handled in a SOC environment.

SOC analysts must:
- Investigate suspicious emails and alerts  
- Identify phishing indicators  
- Escalate confirmed threats  
- Support containment after user interaction  
- Reduce future risk through detection and awareness  

### Example SOC Impact
- Suspicious sender and link mismatch → phishing alert  
- Credential submission → possible account compromise  
- BEC attempt → financial fraud risk  

Understanding phishing indicators improves alert triage, reduces false positives, and strengthens overall security posture.
